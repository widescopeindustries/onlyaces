# Only Aces Operator Memo

## Core thesis

Only Aces is not a lake business. It is a premium, skill-based jackpot challenge business whose real engine is:

- a visible headline prize
- a challenge shot people understand immediately
- repeat-attempt psychology
- unit economics that can heavily favor the operator

The original floating-green format was memorable, but natural water appears to have acted as a scaling bottleneck. Recreating the water-target feel on land removes the site chokehold without sacrificing the emotional hook.

## Why the idea matters now

The U.S. golf market is large, growing, and no longer limited to traditional course play.

- The National Golf Foundation said total U.S. golf participation reached **47.2 million in 2024**, with **28.1 million** on-course golfers.
- The NGF then said total participation climbed to **more than 48 million in 2025**, while on-course participation surpassed **29 million**.
- Texas remains a major golf state. NGF's Texas one-pager showed roughly **1.74 million golfers** in 2020 and **715 facilities** statewide.

This matters because Only Aces is not trying to create golf demand from nothing. It is trying to package existing golf behavior into a prize-driven premium challenge format.

## Why North Texas and why Lucas

North Texas is unusually attractive because it combines golf density, affluence, visibility, and brand adjacency.

- The City of Frisco says the PGA of America officially opened its headquarters there in **August 2022** and that the campus is surrounded by championship courses, a lighted short course, retail, and the Omni PGA Frisco resort.
- D Magazine reported in **April 2024** that North Texas is home to **almost 40 professional golfers**, including around **20 PGA Tour pros** in and around Dallas.
- U.S. Census QuickFacts lists Lucas city median household income at **$221,364** in 2024 dollars, median owner-occupied home value at **$941,900**, and bachelor's degree attainment at **65.1%**.

Lucas is therefore compelling not just as a parcel, but as a flagship proof point inside one of the strongest golf-adjacent consumer corridors in the country.

## Why the flagship should not be a faceless kiosk

The long-term model may support remote monitoring and high automation. The first location probably should not optimize for that.

The flagship should optimize for:

- identity
- credibility
- story
- local energy
- operating learning

That makes a face-led flagship stronger than a sterile autonomous box.

## Why the concept does not need to be led by a golfer

This concept does not need a golf purist at the top. It needs a business builder who understands customer psychology, site economics, margin, branding, and replication.

This is less a golf business than a business built on golf behavior.

## Pro, influencer, and content upside

The base case is retail challenge revenue. The second layer is programming and content.

If the flagship works, the venue can add:

- live leaderboard programming
- Friday night pro or elite-amateur challenge sessions
- documented aces and near misses
- earned media from local golf personalities
- sponsor-backed special events

That means the flagship can evolve from an attraction into a repeatable content-and-event format.

## Current stateside market read

The U.S. market is not empty, but it is fragmented.

- `Sink or Win` markets a Texas floating-green challenge and says it is launching across DFW, but it is still a water-based course-partner model.
- `TheMoneyShot.golf` positions itself as a fully automated floating-island challenge, but it still depends on water hazards and floating-green placements.
- `Chip 2 Win` explicitly markets mobile, event, and permanent venue partnerships for hosts with existing water features.

The pattern is consistent: stateside concepts exist, but they still appear to rely on water, pop-up deployment, or existing course/marina infrastructure. That strengthens the argument that the real white space is not "floating green in America." It is "portable prize-shot flagship without needing a lake."

## MVP recommendation

The MVP should be a single-lane flagship challenge, not a mini driving range.

Recommended first expression:

- 3 to 4 climate-controlled hitting bays
- one signature **111-yard** challenge shot
- one illuminated **10-foot stock tank**
- one **4-foot floating synthetic island green**
- camera coverage for verification and content
- automated ball dispensing
- clear, premium challenge pricing

## Capex posture

There are really two cost conversations:

1. **Lean pilot / proof rig**
   - A simpler private-land test setup that proves shot difficulty, pricing, target design, and scoring workflow.
   - Directionally: **$40k-$90k** if major permanent civil work and full commercial netting are deferred.

2. **Public-facing flagship MVP**
   - A real first site with conditioned bays, commercial dispenser, commercial-grade netting, cameras, lighting, electrical/data, and site prep.
   - Directionally: **$150k-$350k+**, mainly depending on structure finish level and netting scope.

The biggest cost variable is not the stock tank target. It is the safety envelope and the degree of permanency around bays, utilities, and netting.

## Timeline posture

The physical build itself may be fast once materials are on hand, but the real timeline is procurement, site prep, utilities, and approvals.

Practical pre-open range:

- Lean private pilot: **4-8 weeks**
- Public flagship MVP: **8-16+ weeks**

## Strategic conclusion

The Only Aces opportunity is compelling because it reframes the problem correctly.

The prize structure is the business.
The lake was the bottleneck.
The flagship is the proof.
The repeatable template is the prize.
